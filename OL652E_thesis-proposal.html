Imagining Beyond Parameters

AI, Futures Consciousness, and Deeper Sustainability Interventions in Nordic SMEs

Section 1

Background

Nordic small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) are central to the sustainability transition. Constituting 99.9% of all enterprises and contributing around 40% of value-added in Sweden alone, they are indispensable actors for advancing the Sustainable Development Goals (Tsvetkova et al., 2020). Yet their sustainability efforts remain largely surface-level, clustering around energy monitoring and compliance rather than engaging with root causes to redesign business models or relationships with ecological systems (Abson et al., 2017; Tsvetkova et al., 2020). The emerging adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) reinforces this pattern. AI-based solutions predominantly serve this shallow orientation by automating tasks, optimising operations, and reducing costs rather than helping leaders envision what could be fundamentally different (Badghish & Soomro, 2024).

Fuller (1968) anticipated this challenge, arguing that humanity's survival depends on comprehensively anticipatory design science to build fundamentally different solutions. Whether emerging AI solutions are used to imagine new systemic possibilities or merely to reinforce existing efficiencies depends on the orientations of the leaders who deploy them. Currently, AI adoption patterns show a shallow orientation, as they are primarily driven by desires for operational and economic efficiency gains (Badghish & Soomro, 2024). The leverage points framework developed by Meadows (1999) helps explain this pattern by identifying twelve places to intervene in a complex system, ordered by their potential to create transformative change. Shallow leverage points target parameters and material flows, improving efficiency without altering the system's overall trajectory (Abson et al., 2017; Meadows, 1999).

According to Donella Meadows (1999), deep leverage points target the rules, goals, and paradigms from which systems arise, enabling genuine structural transformation. Abson et al. (2017) demonstrated that sustainability interventions overwhelmingly cluster at shallow leverage points and condensed Meadows' twelve points into four system characteristics: parameters, feedback, design, and intent. This classification has been successfully operationalised for empirical research to analyze interactions across levels through "chains of leverage" (Riechers et al., 2022). The critical question is not whether AI can serve sustainability but whether sustainability leaders can use AI to intervene beyond parameters to impact the deeper design and intent of our systems.

This study proposes that imagination is the missing variable. Ahvenharju et al. (2018) conceptualised Futures Consciousness (FC) as a multidimensional psychological capacity comprising time perspective, agency beliefs, openness to alternatives, systems perception, and concern for others. Lalot et al. (2020) validated a psychometric instrument for FC across three European languages, demonstrating structural reliability and predictive validity for sustainability-related engagement. Fischer and Riechers (2019) argued that leveraging deeper system points requires new ways of perceiving systems, capacities that map closely onto FC's dimensions. No study has directly tested whether FC predicts the leverage depth of sustainability interventions, yet the foundational pieces are in place: a validated measure of imaginative capacity (Lalot et al., 2020), an operationalised framework for classifying intervention depth (Riechers et al., 2022), and a theoretical argument connecting the two (Fischer & Riechers, 2019). If FC enables the cognitive capacity to imagine beyond efficiency, then AI tools that amplify this imagination could help leaders target deeper leverage points (Badghish & Soomro, 2024).

Uhl-Bien and Arena's (2017) complexity leadership framework distinguishes administrative leadership, which manages existing structures, from enabling leadership, which creates conditions for adaptive responses to emerge. Deploying AI for parameter-level sustainability is administrative work. Using AI to reimagine business models and value systems requires enabling leadership that fosters emergence, which aligns with what Gopel (2016) described as transformative capacity and paradigm shifts essential for sustainability transitions.

The Nordic SME context is particularly significant because SME managers lack the dedicated specialist departments of large corporations and rely heavily on external tools to shape strategic responses (Neirotti et al., 2018). This creates a leverage gap, where the institutional environment demands transformation yet equips leaders primarily for optimisation. Understanding how some leaders bridge this gap through the interplay of imagination and technology could inform both policy and practice, contributing to SDG 9 by investigating how innovation targets systemic transformation and to SDG 4 by identifying imaginative competencies needed in an AI-mediated landscape (United Nations, 2015; Wiek et al., 2011).

Purpose

This study investigates how Nordic SME sustainability leaders use imagination and AI. It seeks to understand how they create pathways toward deeper sustainability interventions. The research explores the practices through which leaders deploy AI tools. It asks how they move beyond parameter-level optimisation toward systemic redesign. Finally, it explores the association between futures consciousness, AI use patterns, and the leverage depth of sustainability initiatives.

Section 2

Previous Studies

Research on AI, imagination, and sustainability transformation spans several largely isolated literatures. This section reviews six key studies and positions the present study in relation to them.

Badghish and Soomro (2024) investigated predictors of AI adoption for sustainable business performance in SMEs using the Technology-Organization-Environment framework. Their results showed that relative advantage, compatibility, government support, market demand, and sustainable human capital predicted adoption. However, they operationalised sustainable performance primarily as economic and operational efficiency, what Abson et al. (2017) would classify as the shallowest leverage points. The present study builds on their methodology but extends the dependent variable to systemic leverage depth.

Neirotti et al. (2018) surveyed 284 Italian SMEs and established that ICT capability development depends on the combined effect of firm size and competitive environment. Neither this nor Badghish and Soomro's (2024) study considered whether leaders' imaginative capacities shaped technology deployment. The present study addresses this gap directly.

Lalot et al. (2020) developed and validated the Futures Consciousness Scale across three European languages (n = 1,301), using exploratory and confirmatory factor analyses. They demonstrated that the five FC dimensions form a reliable construct and that higher FC predicts engagement with future oriented social behaviours such as pro-environmental and civic actions. The present study uses their instrument to measure FC in Nordic SME leaders.

Abson et al. (2017) aggregated Meadows' twelve leverage points into four tractable levels: parameters, feedback, design, and intent, arguing that sustainability interventions overwhelmingly cluster at shallow leverage points. This conceptual foundation grounds the present study's investigation of what enables leaders to intervene at deeper levels.

Riechers et al. (2022) operationalised the leverage points perspective for empirical research, introducing "chains of leverage" analysis to understand how interventions interact across systemic depths. The present study applies their framework for coding qualitative data and constructing the survey's dependent variable.

Fischer and Riechers (2019) argued that leveraging deep system points requires bridging causal and teleological explanations and engaging with human intent, paradigms, and worldviews. This theoretical argument connects leverage depth to cognitive capacities and directly informs the hypothesis that futures consciousness mediates the relationship between AI use and leverage depth.

Collectively, these studies establish that SME AI adoption is shaped by environmental and organisational factors (Badghish & Soomro, 2024; Neirotti et al., 2018), that FC predicts sustainability engagement (Lalot et al., 2020; Lalot et al., 2021), that interventions cluster at shallow leverage points (Abson et al., 2017), and that deeper intervention requires different cognitive capacities (Fischer & Riechers, 2019). What remains unexamined is whether leaders with stronger futures consciousness use AI differently, and whether this manifests as interventions at deeper leverage points.

Section 3

Methods and Potential Research Questions

This topic can be studied from both a qualitative and quantitative perspective. Below, each perspective is presented with two research questions, methods for data gathering and data analysis, and an overview of the research process.

3.1. Qualitative Perspective

RQ1

How do Nordic SME sustainability leaders use AI tools? How are these tools used in the process of envisioning and implementing sustainability interventions at deeper leverage points?

RQ2

What distinguishes the imagination-AI interaction in SMEs that operate at deeper leverage points (design or intent level)? How does this differ from SMEs that use AI but remain at parameter-level interventions?

Data gathering. A multiple case study will examine four to six Nordic SMEs selected through purposive sampling for demonstrated engagement with sustainability at deeper leverage points combined with active AI tool use, identified through sustainability awards, B Corp databases, and network recommendations. A screening questionnaire will map interventions to the four leverage levels (Riechers et al., 2022). Replication logic will guide selection: two to three cases at deeper leverage points and two to three representing surface-level AI adoption, enabling cross-case comparison (Yin, 2018). Data will be gathered through document analysis and semi-structured interviews (Tsvetkova et al., 2020).

Data analysis. Researchers will conduct a thematic analysis. This process will follow a rigorous six-phase approach (Braun & Clarke, 2006). The analysis will combine both deductive and inductive coding (Braun & Clarke, 2006). Deductive codes will be drawn from the four leverage levels and the five Futures Consciousness (FC) dimensions (Ahvenharju et al., 2018; Riechers et al., 2022). Inductive coding will capture emergent themes directly from the data (Braun & Clarke, 2006). Cross-case analysis will identify patterns. It will specifically explore how imagination-AI interactions differ between cases operating at deeper versus shallower leverage points.

3.2. Quantitative Perspective

RQ3

To what extent are futures consciousness and the leverage depth of AI use associated with the leverage depth of Nordic SME sustainability initiatives? Does this hold when controlling for firm size, sector, and country?

RQ4

Do specific dimensions of futures consciousness (e.g., openness to alternatives, systems perception) show stronger associations with leverage depth than others? Does the combination of high FC and deep AI use explain additional variance beyond their independent effects?

Data gathering. A cross-sectional online survey will target sustainability managers in Nordic SMEs. The target sample is 100 to 150 respondents. The survey instrument will include the validated 20-item Futures Consciousness Scale (Lalot et al., 2020). This scale measures five dimensions: time perspective, agency beliefs, openness to alternatives, systems perception, and concern for others (Ahvenharju et al., 2018; Lalot et al., 2020). The scale has established psychometric properties and has also demonstrated stability across crisis conditions during the COVID-19 pandemic (Lalot et al., 2021). The survey also captures demographics, AI use frequency, and vignettes mapping leverage depth. Following Boateng et al. (2018), five to eight SME managers will evaluate the instrument's measurement validity through cognitive pilot interviews.

Data analysis. Descriptive statistics will map the distribution of FC scores, AI use patterns, and leverage depth across the sample. Correlation analysis will test the associations between FC dimensions, AI use leverage depth, and initiative leverage depth. Hierarchical regression will test whether FC and AI use patterns are associated with leverage depth after controlling for firm size, sector, and country.

Section 4

Practical Contribution

For Nordic SME sustainability managers, the findings would shift a familiar question. Instead of asking "Does this tool improve our sustainability performance?", leaders could ask, "Is this tool helping me optimise what already exists, or helping me imagine and redesign what could?" The case studies would offer concrete models of how leaders combine imagination with AI in practice. The survey findings would reveal which dimensions of futures consciousness associate most strongly with deeper intervention, giving leaders a clearer sense of where to develop their own capacities.

For policymakers and industry associations, the study surfaces conditions under which AI becomes a tool for transformation rather than optimisation, with direct implications for how sustainability training, AI adoption guidance, and innovation support are designed across the Nordic region.

For sustainability researchers, the study moves the leverage points framework (Meadows, 1999; Riechers et al., 2022) from diagnosing where interventions land to understanding what enables leaders to go deeper. It also bridges futures consciousness research (Ahvenharju et al., 2018) with technology adoption literature, asking how a cognitive capacity studied at the individual level plays out in organisational practice when mediated by AI.

More broadly, the study reframes the AI-sustainability relationship. The question is not what AI tools can do, but what human imagination enables leaders to do with them. This contributes to SDG 9 by investigating how innovation targets systemic transformation and to SDG 4 by identifying imaginative competencies needed in an AI-mediated landscape (United Nations, 2015; Wiek et al., 2011).
